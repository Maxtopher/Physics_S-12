Assignment 2: 2D Design & Cutting

Learning how to use Fusion 360, creating an assembly of multiple components, and modeling components from the kit.

Fusion 360 Tutorial

Fusion 360 Tutorial

I went through a fairly basic Fusion 360 introductory tutorial. It taught the basic functionality of the primary buttons and how to use them. It was fairly straightforward and short, but it was a good introduction to the program. It was sufficient for me to be able to start a simple modeling project and really start to learn how to use Fusion 360.

Notched Piece Assembly

I learn best by doing, so to actually practice, understand and experiment with the new skills learned in the tutorial, I completed the notched piece assembly. We began this process in class, but I went back to really try to understand the slightly more complicated sketching tools, like the mirror and offset. I also tried to use the parameter function anywhere I could to ensure my shapes were uniform. From here, extruding and filleting the shape was easy. Next, I copied my pieces and created an assembly of multiple components. I had to adjust the depth of the notches so they wouldn't overlap, but as they now fit together well, I took their projections and put them on a sheet ready to be laser cut.

Modeling the Stepper Motor

I chose the stepper motor for my first model, becasue it's boxy design looked like an ideal starting point for my first model. The first thing I discovered was that all the dimensions were slightly different. Measuing with the calipers confirmed that it was not perfectly square; similar pieces were not identicle and symetric objects were slightly offline. Clearly the tolerance in creating this motor was not too small. However, in order to ensure my sketch was as accurate as possible, I measured and inputed all the dimensions I could. With this model, I initially tried to make one sketch with all the details I would need to extrude the entire model. While this eventually worked, I learned that making more sketches would be much more manageable for future designs. I experimented with adding threading to the screw holes, inserting pre-modeled screws from the "McMaster-Carr Component" database, and tried to add accurate materials using the appearance features. In the end, I think my model came out as a fairly accurate representation of the actual object.

Modeling the USB Battery

While the USB Battery looked fairly simple, I thought that the oddly shaped top would pose an interesting challenge. Creating the general cylindrical body of the battery was straightforward, and adding the USB ports on the bottom were not too challenging. As expected, the top of the battery was much more of a challenge. The semicircle handle on top was created using the rotate tool, but the uneven dimensions of the top were very challenging to model. I was able to use the intersect tool within extrude to get as close as I could to the top of the battery, although it is not perfect. Likewise, the string attatched to the top of the battery took a little experimenting to get right. For this, I used the pipe tool and duplicated the component. When it was finally complete, it turned out to be a pretty good model of the charger, although not perfect at the top.

To create my component assembly, I created a short wire to connect the two devices. This was modeled off of the wire pre-attached to the stepper motor in the kit. The wire was the best way I could think of to combine my two components into an assembly.
